4. Justice issues

4.1 The meaning of justice for Ukrainians

Key Finding

Punishment of those guilty of crimes is the most important aspect of justice for nearly half of Ukrainians (47%), followed by establishing the truth about all events (30%). This reflects a strong desire for accountability that aligns with Ukraine's position that accountability for crimes of aggression and war crimes should be non-negotiable.

Source: Rating Group, March 2024

When asked what justice regarding Russia's aggression against Ukraine personally means to them, Ukrainians express clear priorities:

Punishment of perpetrators is paramount, with 47% of respondents identifying this as their primary understanding of justice. This strong emphasis on accountability aligns with Ukraine's negotiating position that prosecution of war crimes and crimes of aggression cannot be compromised.

Establishing the truth about events ranks second at 30%, indicating that documentation and acknowledgment of what happened is also considered an essential component of justice.

Material compensation, though important at 17%, ranks third, suggesting that while reparations matter, they are viewed as secondary to accountability and truth-seeking.

The minimal percentage of respondents who found it difficult to answer (3%) or chose "other" options (3%) indicates that Ukrainians have clear and well-formed conceptions of what justice should entail in this context.

4.2 The necessity of bringing perpetrators to justice

Key Finding

Three-quarters of Ukrainians (75%) believe justice cannot be achieved without the arrest and prosecution of perpetrators of aggression and war crimes, with 50% stating that justice would be completely impossible without accountability. This underscores the centrality of criminal accountability in Ukrainian conceptions of justice.

Source: Rating Group, March 2024

The data reveals an overwhelming consensus among Ukrainians on the necessity of legal accountability for perpetrators:

A striking 75% believe justice cannot be achieved without bringing perpetrators to justice, with 50% stating it would be "not at all" possible and 25% saying it would be "rather" not possible.

Only 22% believe justice could be achieved without arresting and prosecuting perpetrators (9% "definitely yes" and 13% "rather yes"), while just 3% found it difficult to answer.

This strong consensus explains why Ukraine's government has maintained accountability as a non-negotiable element in peace talks. The absence of questions about accountability in most surveys about negotiations likely reflects this settled consensus in Ukrainian society.

The data suggests that any peace framework that fails to include robust mechanisms for legal accountability would face significant challenges in gaining acceptance among the Ukrainian population.

4.3 Justice priorities for Ukrainian government and society

Key Finding

Ukrainians identify bringing perpetrators to justice (55%) and removing collaborators from government (46%) as the top priorities for ensuring justice. This focus on accountability and governance integrity takes precedence over material concerns like infrastructure restoration (14%) or property compensation (12%).

Source: Rating Group, March 2024

When asked about government and societal priorities for ensuring justice, Ukrainians demonstrate a clear hierarchy of concerns:

Accountability mechanisms dominate the top priorities, with bringing perpetrators to justice (55%) and removing collaborators from government (46%) ranking first and second. This suggests Ukrainians view justice primarily through the lens of addressing those responsible for wrongdoing.

Humanitarian concerns form a secondary cluster of priorities, including finding missing persons (30%), establishing facts of crimes (24%), and obtaining financial compensation (23%).

International pressure mechanisms such as strengthening sanctions (20%) also receive significant support, showing the recognition of external tools for achieving justice.

Material reconstruction priorities—including infrastructure restoration (14%), property compensation (12%), and medical assistance (10%)—rank lower, suggesting Ukrainians view physical rebuilding as important but secondary to addressing the human dimensions of justice.

The relative ranking of these priorities reinforces the brief's assessment that accountability for crimes is non-negotiable for Ukrainians, and explains why this issue has remained central to Ukraine's negotiating position despite the significant material destruction caused by the war.

4.4 Implications for negotiation approaches

The data on justice issues reveals several important implications for negotiation approaches:

Accountability as a core demand: The consistent prioritization of bringing perpetrators to justice—across multiple survey questions—confirms that accountability mechanisms must be central to any peace framework. This helps explain why Ukraine has consistently maintained that prosecution for crimes of aggression and war crimes is non-negotiable.

Multiple dimensions of justice: While punishment of perpetrators is paramount, Ukrainians also value truth-seeking, addressing collaborators, finding missing persons, and obtaining compensation. This suggests an effective justice approach within negotiations needs to be comprehensive rather than narrowly focused.

Institutional requirements: The data supports Ukraine's position that independent, international, and national legal mechanisms are needed. The high priority given to accountability implies that ad hoc or symbolic justice measures would likely be insufficient for Ukrainian society.

Material versus moral justice: The relatively lower ranking of material concerns (compensation, reconstruction) compared to accountability suggests that economic inducements alone would be insufficient to balance an absence of meaningful accountability measures.

Timing considerations: The expectation that perpetrators should face justice "in the coming years" suggests that while Ukrainians demand accountability, there may be some flexibility on immediate implementation if credible mechanisms and commitments are established.

Non-negotiable status: The overwhelming consensus that justice requires accountability (75%) explains why this issue has largely been absent from surveys about potential compromises—unlike territorial or security issues where public opinion has shown more flexibility. For most Ukrainians, accountability appears to be a requirement for justice rather than an optional element to be negotiated.
