7. Issues of Ukrainian domestic policy

7.1 Elections and timing

Key Finding

A strong majority (62%) of Ukrainians believe elections should not be held until the war formally ends, while only 14% support holding elections as soon as possible. This supports the brief's assertion that "Ukrainians are clearly against any elections before the fighting is over and martial law is lifted."

Source: Ipsos/The Economist, March 2025

7.2 Support for different election types during war

Key Finding

Strong opposition to holding elections during wartime is evident across all election types, with presidential elections facing the highest opposition (60%). Even a referendum on ending the war faces substantial opposition (38%), confirming Ukrainians' preference to wait until after the war.

Source: IRI, September-October 2024

While all election types face net opposition during war, a referendum about ending the war receives the most support (54%), suggesting Ukrainians see value in public consultation specifically on peace terms.

Presidential elections face the strongest opposition (60%), aligning with the emphasis on postponing elections until proper conditions exist.

Across all election types, strong opposition consistently outweighs strong support, indicating firm conviction rather than mild preference for postponing elections.

7.3 Presidential legitimacy

Source: Ipsos/The Economist, March 2025

Despite the delayed elections, 80% of Ukrainians believe President Zelensky maintains the same democratic legitimacy as before his term's originally scheduled expiration. This widespread recognition of constitutional continuity during wartime helps explain why most Ukrainians prioritize ending the war over holding elections.

7.4 Language policy

Key Finding

A majority of Ukrainians (54%) oppose accepting Russian as a language in some parts of the country to end the war, confirming the assertion that making Russian a second state language is "clearly non-admissible" for Ukrainians. However, up to 41% could potentially accept Russian as a second state language in certain contexts.

Source: Ipsos/The Economist, March 2025
Source: KIIS, March 2025

When asked directly if Ukraine should "accept Russian language in some parts of the country to end the war," 54% disagree while 29% agree, showing clear majority opposition.

However, when asked about accepting "Russian as a second state language" as part of a broader peace package, acceptability rises to 41%, though opposition remains higher at 48%.

By contrast, 55% can accept "Ukrainian as the only state language" with only 32% opposed, indicating stronger preference for Ukrainian-only status.

7.5 Decision-making autonomy

Key Finding

A clear majority of Ukrainians (59%) support their country making decisions independently, while only 37% would accept coordination with Russia. This demonstrates a strong preference for maintaining full Ukrainian sovereignty in decision-making.

Source: KIIS, March 2025
