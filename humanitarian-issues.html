5. Humanitarian issues

5.1 Awareness of civilian captivity

Key Finding

A large majority of Ukrainians (78%) are aware of the issue of civilian captivity, with 27% being well aware and 51% having heard something about it. This high level of awareness supports the brief's assertion that the return of civilian captives represents a widespread public consensus rather than a concern limited to specific advocacy groups.

Source: Rating Group, March 2025

5.2 Sources of knowledge about civilian captivity

Key Finding

Information about prisoner exchanges (55%) and general awareness of the phenomenon (48%) dominate Ukrainians' knowledge of civilian captivity. This suggests that while the issue has high visibility, many Ukrainians lack detailed information about the scale of the problem or specific government and international initiatives.

Source: Rating Group, March 2025

5.3 Personal connection to civilian captivity

Key Finding

A significant minority of Ukrainians (16%) report having friends or acquaintances who are or were in civilian captivity. This translates to approximately one in six Ukrainians having personal connections to the issue, underscoring its widespread impact across Ukrainian society.

Source: Rating Group, March 2025

5.4 Responsibility for returning civilian captives

Key Finding

An overwhelming majority of Ukrainians (88%) believe that state bodies of Ukraine should be responsible for deciding on the return of civilian hostages. This aligns with the brief's assertion that "the government is expected to prioritise the return of all captives," reflecting clear public consensus on institutional responsibility.

Source: Rating Group, March 2025

5.5 Implications for negotiation approaches

The data on civilian captivity issues reveals several important implications for negotiation approaches:

Broad-based societal concern: With 78% awareness and 16% having personal connections to the issue, the return of civilian captives clearly represents a widespread public priority rather than a narrow interest. This supports the brief's characterization of these demands reflecting "widespread public consensus."

Clear institutional responsibility: The overwhelming support (88%) for state bodies taking the lead on captive returns indicates that Ukrainians expect their government, not international mediators or civil society, to be the primary advocate for captives in negotiations. This aligns with the brief's statement that "the government is expected to prioritise the return of all captives."

Knowledge gaps and communication opportunities: While awareness of the issue is high, detailed knowledge about numbers of captives and specific initiatives is limited. This suggests potential value in more robust public communication about the scale of civilian captivity and government efforts to address it.

Multi-stakeholder approach: Although state leadership is paramount, significant minorities recognize roles for international organizations, civil society, and families. This suggests Ukrainians favor a coordinated approach that leverages multiple channels of influence rather than relying exclusively on state-to-state negotiations.

Non-negotiable status: The combination of high awareness, significant personal connections, and clear expectations of state responsibility supports the brief's assessment that prisoner return is considered a "non-negotiable component of any acceptable peace deal." Negotiators should recognize that concessions on this issue would likely face substantial domestic resistance.
